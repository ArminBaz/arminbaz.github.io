What do I want, pt. 1

This blog was originally supposed to be a place where I can get my feelings out in a somewhat "public" place. I'm not even sure why I feel the need to have this be out there. Of course I could write this in a notebook or type it on a hidden document on my laptop, but there's a different feeling that comes with the knowledge that someone may stumble upon this. In a way, I feel like the underground man. The underlying what if motivates me in a somewhat perverse way to more fully flesh out my thoughts and be sure that I completely agree with what I am putting down. In many ways, I think this is a good thing. If I am to ponder about what I want to do with my life and why I want to do certain things, it is best to flesh it out to the finest detail is it not?

Now that I gave such a dramatic introduction anything else would feel wrong. I'll begin about what I want to study for my PhD (if I'm fortunate enough to get accepted anywhere) and why I even feel the need to get PhD in the first place.

I was thinking, I have been thinking, quite a lot about what I want to study if I were to get a PhD. I know what I like. I know that I really enjoy machine learning, specifically deep learning. I like electrical engineering, but mostly when it is in line with machine learning (signal processing), some embedded systems and robotics. Neuroscience, but not when it gets too into the nitty gritty of the chemistry and behind it. And psychology.

Now what do you get when you combine all of the above? Nothing really in particular. There doesn't seem to be any specific field that is a pure combination of everything I mentioned. However, there seems to be a growing body of research on combining engineering, neuroscience, and deep learning, in a myriad of ways, to study the mind and also as a way to further each of the respective afformentioned fields. This would include anyone at the Center for Brains Minds and Machines (CBMM), professor Dan Yammins from Stanford, Niftali Tishby in Israel, Christof Koch at the Allen Institute, among many others.

So it sounds like it might be possible to find some combination of everything? Sort of. What I have seen with most of these labs is that their research vaguely involves everything. The research is really interesting though. For example, a recent paper (one that I have provided a read through of) combines neuroscience and deep learning by using our understanding of the structure of our visual system to aid in design of an ANN that is more architecturally correct. Why? I think it is because they think that with the advent of a more structurally significant neural network, they will be able to decipher some meaning about our brain. This idea is not so far fetched either. It has been shown that networks that perform well on ImageNet have a similar interaction to unseen images that our brains do.

Unfortunately, the reality is that most of the time these labs are not doing this kind of research. Most of the time they are either doing some research in neuroscience or some research in deep learning. This interesting combination of the two is rare. On top of that all the labs that do this kind of research are either on the east coast or too far up the west coast. I want to try and stay more central to southern california so the schools that I have really been looking into are USC, UCLA, and Caltech. I can safely cross Caltech off the list because the chances of me getting in there are about as good as me getting through Buds. UCLA has some pretty interesting research in this area (Prof. Jonathan Kao / Brain Research Institute). USC is somewhat dry. The only really cool deep learning lab here is CLVR (which I am highly considering becoming a part of), LONI (which I am currently a part of), and the Brain and Creativity Institute.

Obviously, before I continue, I need to look through all of the professors and construct and actual list for myself of people that I would consider doing research under. There are a lot of factors. The most important one is of course the research that is being done, if I am not 100% into it then I can kiss my mental health goodbye. The second most important factor is the lab itself. I don't think I would enjoy working in a lab that is predominantly made up of foreigners (Chinese, Indian). The culture difference and the language barrier are enough to prevent me from ever having meaningful relationships with them. The third factor is the professors age. Why? I have noticed that most (not all) older professors are more or less set in their ways and make little room for current research methods. On top of that I feel like a younger professors lab is more like a start up. Everyone is young, there is one "boss" but the environment is more less open with less class dynamics, and everything we are working on is new, cutting edge, and exciting.

So, I have pretty much accepted at this point in my life that I want to get a PhD. I know that the research has to be deep learning focused and anything else is really icing on the cake at this point. Honestly, I feel like as long as (1) it is with a relatively young professor and (2) It is a school in southern California. I will be more than happy wherever that may be. I know this might sound like it contradicts my previous paragraph where I talked about the type of research I would like to be a part of. But if I were to be completely honest anything that is related to deep learning sounds really cool to me. Obviously I would love to somehow use that to further our understanding of consciousness and/or emotions and feelings. However, I have to be honest with myself and I have to realize that the labs that are doing this are far and few between. On top of that, I am not even sure if the labs that are doing this sort of thing are even providing much utility. Either way, I need to do more research and really dedicate some time to this. (New blog post?)

Now, I feel like I should address the why. Why do I even want a PhD? That is the million dollar question. To be completely honest I am not sure if we can pinpoint our reasoning for doing anything that we do. I say this for our choices and actions that aren't inherent to our survival. To the things we do choose to do out of our own "free will". There is no such thing. We are the creation of our past experiences everything that has happened has led to this point. Even if we don't live in a purely deterministic world, the people we surround ourselves with and our environment will influence our decisions too. Had I not had two parents who pursued doctorate degrees I might not have been inclined to do so myself. Had I not gotten into research and realized that I would much rather do that for a fraction of the pay than work an engineering job purely for money I would most likely not be writing this either. The point is, I don't see much of a point to dwell on our choices and think about if it is the right one every step of the way. In fact, had I adopted this philsophy to life, I would most likely be sitting in my room, motionless and decisionless as I would constantly be second guessing myself.

Sure, getting a PhD might not give me the most long term payoff in terms of money. But life isn't all about money. As far as I am currently concerned, research and gaining knowledge in things that interest me are my main concerns for the unforseeable future. I want to maximize this. Following this logic I don't see why I shouldn't get a PhD in the first place. The whole point of a doctorate degree is to make you an "expert" in your field. Not only that, it also trains you to be a researcher. It trains you how to think like a scientist, how to approach problems using the scientific method (a lost art form), and it is a validation of sorts for future employeers that says "I am a scientist". A PhD, to me, is most definitely a worthwhile investment if you plan on staying in academia or want to continue into industry as a research scientist.

On that note, I will conclude this blog post. I really want to start looking into different labs (again), maybe I missed the perfect one. Updates to come.
