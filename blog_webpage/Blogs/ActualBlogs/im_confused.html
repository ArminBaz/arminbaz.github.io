I'm Confused

Here comes one of the most intense word vomits of all time, so if anyone is actually reading this... brace yourselves.

The purpose of this blog post is for me to try and actually figure out where my interests lie. I can sit around and muse about it all day, but I've noticed that the best way for me to reach a conclusion is to either talk to somebody or write. Both are convenient methods for one to organize their thoughts into a semi-coherent format, so hopefully by the end of this blog post I feel like I have reached some sort of conclusion.

I would say I have more than a passing interest in two main fields. The first being computational neuroscience and the second being artificial intelligence. Now, because both are such broad fields it would be prudent to try and narrow my scope of interest a bit. So, I would say as far as computational neuroscience is concerned I believe I am mainly interested in understanding how the regions of the brain do the things that they do. How does our visual cortex allow us to make out a hand? How does our pre-frontal cortex allow for complex planning and decision making? Within artificial intelligence my main interest is in deep learning. I think deep learning is so cool, maybe because of the fact that it was brain-inspired. I'm not sure. Within that space, I'm mainly interested in applying theories and ideas from neuroscience to make better deep learning models. By better I mean more efficient in the amount of data it needs and more general in how it can generalize to unseen data and completely new, yet loosely related, tasks that it has never seen before. But now bodes the question, "what do I enjoy more?". Do I have a greater desire to uncover marginal secrets about the brain that propably won't be read by anybody and most likely won't lead to any real breakthroughs in neuroscience and our understanding of the brain? Or do I instead desire to make marginally better machine learning models that probably won't actually be used by anybody in the real world and will be completely forgotten about in a few days?

The reason I phrase things the way I do is because I think it is best to be realistic with oneself. Objectively, I don't think there is a high probability that I will have a publication with a high impact no matter what path I choose. I thought that perhaps by framing it in the least desirable way, I might be able to hone in on my true interest. It hasn't helped yet, so far both still sound equally interesting to me. Although, the more I type, the more I can't help but think that it is the comp. neuro route that I really want to pursue. For some reason, I have a great desire to follow in the footsteps of people like Terence Sejnowski, David Marr, and Tomaso Poggio. I would consider all three of them to be among the top of my "academic heroes". To me, this sounds like I really want to get into computational/theoretical neuroscience. However, I also can't help but think that perhaps my obsession with these guys is not because they are in computational neuroscience, but rather becuase they are also seminal figures in artificial intelligence and machine learning. Maybe, its because they are more in the theoretical, and that is something that my hubris has made me always want to be a part of. It's hard to tell.

I spent the better part of the whole day today reading through different professor's research and seeing what part of computational neuroscience really interests me. Unfortunately, it was when I came across the researchers at Caltech where everything started spiraling out of control. The neuroscience guys there are a little too into neuroscience for me, and the machine learning people seem really focused on machine learning, but are also really open to applying ideas from different disciplines. This sounds really cool to me, but at the same time, it doesn't? Maybe its because I have this desire to understand the brain a bit more and to not only understand it more, but be a part of the forefront of that research that is attempting to understand it. I think that the reason this area of computational neuroscience research sounds so interesting to me might also be rooted in the fact that I've never actually partaken in it. Therefore, I have a very "starry-eyed" view of this field where I think that I would be uncovering more than I actually am. Again though, I need to assume that I would produce incredibely marginal, borderline meaningless, research no matter what path I choose. At the end of the day the question is do I see myself mathematically modeling the brain? Or do I see myself taking other people's mathematical models and interpretations of the brain, and using that to create better machine learning models and artificial agents?

I think after spending all this time writing, the answer is actually painfully obvious. I have always been in love with machine learning and artificial intelligence. I find pattern recognition as a whole to be such an interesting concept, and I want to be able to imbue this knowledge into computers. Introspecting as much as I can while I am writing this, I think I would regret diving so deeply into the brain that I completely ignore artificial intelligence more than I would regret diving deeply into AI and not touching the brain. I think the main thing for me is that I absolutely must join a lab that is multi-disciplinary. By multi-disciplinary, I mean a lab where I am afforded the creative freedom to deeply learn and explore ideas in neuroscience so that I can apply them to AI. Becuase at the end of the day the purpose of the PhD is for me to learn and be trained as a scientist. Ideally, the lab I work at might even allow me to dive deeper into neuroscience than simply applying concepts. I think that would be quite ideal as well. There are many paths this can take, but it seems like the main thing is that I will have to join a math centered lab either focusing in computational neuroscience or machine learning, and through the common language of mathematics I can find this overlap that I so greatly desire.
