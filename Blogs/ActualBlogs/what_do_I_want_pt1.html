What do I want, pt. 1

This blog was originally supposed to be a place where I can get my feelings out in a somewhat "public" place. I'm not even sure why I feel the need to have this be out there. Of course I could write this in a notebook or type it on a hidden document on my laptop, but there's a different feeling that comes with the knowledge that perhaps someone may stumble upon this. In a way, I feel like the underground man. The knowledge of the underlying what if motivates me in a somewhat perverse way to more fully flesh out my thoughts and be sure that I completely agree with what I am putting down. In many ways, I think this is a good thing. If I am to ponder about what I want to do with my life and why I want to do certain things, it is best to flesh it out to the finest detail is it not?

Now that I gave such a dramatic introduction anything else would feel wrong. I'll begin about what I want to study for my PhD (if I'm fortunate enough to get accepted anywhere) and why I even feel the need to get PhD in the first place.

I was thinking, I have been thinking, quite a lot about what I want to study if I were to get a PhD. I know what I like. I know that I really enjoy machine learning, specifically deep learning. I like electrical engineering, but mostly when it is in line with machine learning (signal processing), some embedded systems and robotics. Neuroscience, but not when it gets too into the nitty gritty of the chemistry and behind it. And psychology.

Now what do you get when you combine all of the above? Nothing really in particular. There doesn't seem to be any specific field that is a pure combination of everything I mentioned. However, there seems to be a growing body of research on combining engineering, neuroscience, and deep learning, in a myriad of ways, to study the mind and also as a way to further each of the respective afformentioned fields. This would include anyone at the Center for Brains Minds and Machines (CBMM), professor Dan Yammins from Stanford, Niftali Tishby in Israel, Christof Koch at the Allen Institute, among many others.

So it sounds like it might be possible to find some combination of everything? Sort of. What I have seen with most of these labs is that their research vaguely involves everything. The research is really interesting though. For example, a recent paper (one that I have provided a read through of) combines neuroscience and deep learning by using our understanding of the structure of our visual system to aid in design of an ANN that is more architecturally correct. Why? I think it is because they think that with the advent of a more structurally significant neural network, they will be able to decipher some meaning about our brain. This idea is not so far fetched either. It has been shown that networks that perform well on ImageNet have a similar interaction to unseen images that our brains do.

Unfortunately, the reality is that most of the time these labs are not doing this kind of research. Most of the time they are either doing some research in neuroscience or some research in deep learning. This interesting combination of the two is rare. On top of that all the labs that do this kind of research are either on the east coast or too far up the west coast. I want to try and stay more central to southern california so the schools that I have really been looking into are USC, UCLA, and Caltech. I can safely cross Caltech off the list because the chances of me getting in there are about as good as me getting through Buds. UCLA has some pretty interesting research in this area (Prof. Jonathan Kao / Brain Research Institute). USC is somewhat dry. The only really cool deep learning lab here is CLVR (which I am highly considering becoming a part of), LONI (which I am currently a part of), and the Brain and Creativity Institute.
