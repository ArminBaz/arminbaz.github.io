What do I want, pt. 3

Alright, so quite a lot of time has passed since I last did one of these blog posts. In that time a lot has changed. I am one course away from completing my masters degree in electrical engineering at USC with a specialization in artificial intelligence and data science. That's dope, but what really changed?

The real change is that I actually have a pretty good gpa now. I'm sitting at around a 3.75 cumulative gpa, with only one class that was taken P/NP. This is good, this puts me above the 3.5 threshold that many PhD programs secretly need in order to seriously look at someones application. On top of that, as I previously said, I only took one class P/NP. I think this would help me in a favorable way because it shows that even though there was a pandemic, I didn't completely take advantage of the situation and take all my courses P/NP, save one that I got an A in, making my gpa a 4.0. Now, of course this isn't fair. I'm sure there are plenty of other students who were severly affected by the pandemic and had to take all their classes P/NP, or even worse, just drop the program all together. But, if there's one thing I learned from my time in school, its that they seriously don't give a fuck.

As anyone could probably have guessed by this point. I have decided that I would like to pursue a PhD. The idea of being at the edge of a field and pushing the boundary slightly further is something that really appeals to me. The main barrier to entry thus far has been my gpa, and now I have that under control. Unfortunately, this doesn't mean I'm in the clear. In order to be seriously competitive, I need a publication, and I need one bad. For every program in the top 30 in cs, it's pretty much expected that the competitive applicants will have 1-2 publications. Not only that, they probably have a publication in a pretty exclusive conference.

Well... that fucking sucks. I get my gpa under control and now I have another mountain to climb. But, to be fair, if I really want a PhD, then I should be able to put in the work to be able to get at least one publication in the year before I apply. I mean, I got a whole year! Also, because I am mostly interested in computer vision, I think that I can get by applying to PhD programs in electrical engineering. Funnily enough, this actually makes things slightly easier. Electrical engineering is actually quite less competitive than CS. This doesn't mean it's guaranteed, especially for a program like CalTech (my dream school), but it does give me some room to breathe.

I actually spent quite a bit of time stalking my dream lab (Computational Vision lab led by Pietro Perona), and it seems like a lot of the student's in there are pretty normal? Obviously they're all super smart, but imo they didn't have crazy stats. They had good gpa's and research experience. Upon further investigation, I also saw that a lot of them had backgrounds in mathematics and electrical engineering. Just like me. I may not have as good of math chops as them, but I think that I can hold my weight. This just showed me that this lab is more focused on quality rather than quantity. If I can show a passion for computer vision research, mathematical ability, and a subset of the field that I would like to apply my research to, I think I just might be able to make it work. On top of all that, I am currently in contact with one of the more senior students in the lab (Sara Beery). Not only is she super nice, but I think she might be able to put in a good word for with the professor. Of course this is a bit of a stretch and I don't want to put her in an awkward position. But, if all goes well, that could be a very useful connection. Even if it doesn't work with the lab, her research is so cool that I'm just happy with the fact that she knows my name lol.

Well, that's it for now. I guess I'll come back in another few months with an update.
