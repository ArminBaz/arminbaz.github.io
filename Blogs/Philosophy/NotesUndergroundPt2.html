Part 2 of Notes from underground

Link to the book.

Part 2:

Chapter 1:

The underground man begins his story by saying that even at the young age of 24 he was gloomy and seclusive, not as much as he currently is, but still enough to the point where people looked at him with a sort of loathing.

The underground man shares his insecurity with us again by talking about how he has always had a feeling of discontent with himself and he, self admittedly, wrongfully assumes this characteristic to others as well. He also talks about how he dislikes his own face, how above all he worries about looking abject and strives for having the face of an intelligent, confident man, whatever that means.

As I continue to read, I continue to resonate with the underground man. His explanations of insecurity and attribution of such to others continues to strike a similar nerve with me. Although it is easy to say that it is not right to assume others to think like this. One cannot help but assume others tend to think like themselves because that is all they know.

The underground man continues by talking about how he dislikes the romanticism of Germany and France, it is too idealistic. Instead, he prefers Russian romanticism where although they are focused on what is good and beautiful in life, they are more realistic.

It seems as if Dostoevsky is, in some sense, writing the underground man to represent Russia. He hates the romanticism that leads him to want to befriend his coworkers, yet he adores Russian romanticism. He becomes friends with his coworkers (lets in western thought), only to cast them aside (become at odds with how much influence western philosophy is affecting him).

One day, depressed and alone, the underground man is walking home when he sees someone being thrown out of a window. Being starved for attention, he decides that he would fancy being thrown out of a window too. Anything is better than being alone. When inside a soldier moves him to the side without any trouble. This sparks a furious desire for revenge.

The desire grew for years, I repeat… years. He stalked him, learned where he lived, how he lived, and with whom he may have lived. His stalking grew to obsession as he saw how the soldier would only move out of the way for higher ranks and no one else. The underground man despised him but was also extremely envious of him at the same time. He soon came up with a master plan.

His plan? To walk directly into the soldier, not get out of his way, and push back hard. He set out by acquiring expensive clothes to look the soldiers equal. The main component, replacing his racoon collar with a German beaver one (more on why I think this is important to come). He eventually gets the courage to do it, but the officer hardly notices.

This whole story, in my opinion, continues to fall in line with Dostoevsky’s commentary on western philosophy coming to Russia. The underground man has too lofty of ideals for Russia and he despises those that can adapt so well to the area as the tough, yet bending, soldier. The collar is important because the underground man talks about how the German beaver collar doesn’t hold up well in the Russian climate, yet it is beautiful while it does. This is again commentary on how western philosophy looks nice and how it may appear beautiful. But it does not translate well to the lifestyle in Russia. Even after he carried it out, the officer hardly noticed his “diabolical” plan that took years for him to think about and act out.

Chapter 2:

After his heroic display of strength and independence in chapter 1. He comes down with what he describes to be a sickness. Now the actual nature of this sickness is debatable and is to be left to the reader to interpret the meaning of. My assumption, the sickness of being underground, being alone, not allowing oneself to truly be vulnerable.

He tells us that he combats this state of degradation by thinking himself to be a hero at times. For a normal man would not accept this. However, a hero knows himself to be greater than a normal man, and thus can defile himself at times without shame.

He describes his dreams, they are western European in origin. Dreams that he describes fondly, yet he cannot bring into the real world.

The underground man’s dreams and his inability to bring them into the real world and act on the feelings that they give him is again Dostoevsky commentating on how western thought does not belong in Russia.

The chapter ends with the underground man talking about how he gets sudden desires to share his genius only to become socially anxious in such situations. In typical fashion, he tells us that all is well and continues to boast about himself.

I am now at odds. At this point it is known that Dostoevsky is using the underground man as a vessel for western European thought as a way to show that it does not belong in Russia. However, what is necessary for displaying the underground man in such a light? Why does it matter that he is depressed and socially anxious.

One answer could be because Dostoevsky really wanted to get across how contemptuous a man can become when one culture tries to adopt another so suddenly. Perhaps these character traits are only put on display for the reader to realize, through obvious dramatization, that we are also victims to the same thinking patterns.

Chapter 3:

The underground man can’t contain himself anymore and visits one of his old friends from school, even though he hated everyone there. On the way there however, he concedes to the reader that even though he despises his old schoolmates, he seeks their respect at the same time.

He describes his old classmate with a sort of stark hatred and resentment. Jealousy because this classmate, Zverkov, was handsome and rich, hatred because he seemed to be aware of this and would “swagger” around the others who could do nothing but bow at his feet. “..simply because he had been favoured by the gifts of nature.”

The three men are planning a party for the friend, none of them acknowledge the underground man, and they make it quite clear that he is not a part of their plans. The underground man however, rudely, interjects himself to be included. After obvious vexation from the others, they concede, and he is allowed to come.

Afterwards, the underground man terribly regrets his actions. He confides in the reader that his reasoning for doing this, which we are aware of at this point, is that he has a desire to put himself in uncomfortable situations. Why? Because that is when he feels truly alive.

The rest of the chapter is the underground man giving us some back story on his time in school, why the other student’s mocked him, how he hates them, how he secretly just wants all of them to like and respect him, the usual stuff.

Chapter 4:

This chapter covered the dinner in which the underground man invited himself to. It was just as painful and hard to read as one would expect it to be. No one wanted him there. Simonov, the friend who arranged it, did not tell the underground man of the sudden time change, perhaps because he was hoping that he would leave after realizing what he had done.

The dinner was awkward and the underground man made unwarranted and rude comments which were all reciprocated with rude behaviour from his dinner companions. In his head, the underground man was constantly thinking to himself how much better he is than all of these guests and how he doesn’t need them. Yet, immediately afterwards he would think about how he wishes to have their respect.

If there is anything this chapter elucidated, it is how other people view the underground man. They respond negatively to him because they have no idea of the inner turmoil he is going through. To them, he is simply making rude remarks for no apparent reason.

This chapter again shows the strange pleasure that the underground man also derives from being at rock bottom. This is inline with his comments from part 1 about not truly living until you experience pain. He derives weird perverse pleasures from being insulted, ridiculed, suffering for no pointless reason. When you willingly subjugate yourself to such things, is when you are truly free.

Chapter 5:

The underground man gets a carriage and tries to follow the party to the brothel. During the ride he is delighting himself with the thought that he will slap one of the men and initiate the fighting. He enjoys the thought of getting beat up because to him it is as if he is getting “revenge” on them.

Once he enters the brothel, they are nowhere to be found and instead he sees a woman that he is instantly attracted to.

Again this chapter shows us how he is really chasing his own freedom. He attempts to live life by forcing himself on others, unfortunately the only way he knows how is through anger and contempt.

Chapter 6:

The underground man wakes up next to the young prostitute Liza. The whole chapter is him essentially lecturing her on different things, feeling powerful and proud of himself because he can elucidate emotions within her.

Liza responds by telling him that his speech “sounds like it’s taken from a book.”

I feel like this is something that many of us are guilty of. When we cannot make casual conversation, we instead opt to simply sit in silence, or go on a long monologue about something. Here, the underground man has chosen to lecture young Liza.

Chapter 7:

Basically a continuation of the last chapter. The underground man continues to tell a fictional story, that he plagiarized, to Liza because he likes this feeling of “emotional power” he has over her. She tells him of a letter that she cherishes from a man who professed his love for her, how she was forced into prostitution to help her family, and how she is hopeful that she will eventually climb the ranks and be able to truly take care of her family.

The underground man shoots down this “lofty notion”, telling her that such dreams are falsely based on a utopian trust of both the societal systems in place and in another man’s ability to not act irrationally.

These statements made by the underground man seem to fall in line with the political themes of Dostoevsky’s time. Again, as Western ideas and philosophy were coming in, there was a great importance being placed on this “utopian ideal”. It is important to note that Russia, at the time, was still predominantly serf based with clear classes.

As Dostoevsky notes that a man like has no other choice than to exist during these times, I wonder if it is because Dostoevsky himself is against these western ideas.

Chapter 8:

The next day, the underground man regrets the fact that he gave Liza his address, he brooded on this for a few minutes before he set his mind towards making amends with the men whose dinner party he crashed.

To make amends he writes a letter where he writes off the events of the night before somewhat casually. How he couldn’t hold his alcohol and how he regrets the way he acted. He gives it a “gentleman's tone” and is very pleased with himself with the way it turned out.

The rest of the chapter is spent with the underground man both dreading Liza’s visit, but at the same time really looking forward to it. He sees himself as her savior, that she will come over and fall in love with him because of his wit and way with words.

He also talks about how much he hates Apollan and how he wishes to exert power over the man by withholding his wages. Yet each time he tries Apollan always “wins” and he gives him the wages within a day or two.

At the end of the chapter Liza finally visits and the underground man is extremely anxious.

Chapter 9:

Liza comes over and the underground man goes on a long disorganized speech about how at the brothel he only wanted to exert control and power over her like the way the others did to him earlier in the night. He tells her how he was anxious of her arrival because he was afraid of her seeing the way he lived and thinking ill of him.

Liza, filled with sympathy, throws her arms around him, crying. The underground man accepts this affection and lays down on the sofa and also cries for some time. Afterwards, he gets up, upset with himself for being so weak, and attempts to again exert power over Liza, however she only sees this as passion and again hugs him.

Sparknotes brought up an interesting point. They said that the underground man has extremely romantic and lofty views of life. Filled with appreciation for the “beautiful and lofty”, officers fighting duels, and prostitutes needing saving. Yet Liza is a heroine worthy of a romantic novel.

Chapter 10:

The underground man attempts to dominate Liza one last time. By using her like the prostitute she is. Liza, however, perceives this as an act of love.

In the last moment, the underground man tells her that he is not capable of love and that he views love as one person wanting to dominate the other. Liza gets up to leave. The underground man in a last attempt to dominate her, throws some money into her hands, to “repay her”. Liza at this point is so disgusted that she immediately leaves the apartment.

The underground man, realizing the horrible mistake he has made, his actual chance of love, chases after her, only to find that she is nowhere to be found.

The underground man ends his notes by saying that most people reading this will resonate with him and realize that they too think like him. Yet, they will never admit that to themselves. In some way, the underground man is living in more ways than many people.
