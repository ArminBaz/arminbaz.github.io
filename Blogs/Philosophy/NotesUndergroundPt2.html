Paper Read Through: Imitating Latent Policies from Observation

Link to the book.

Part 2:

Chapter 1:

The underground man begins his story by saying that even at the young age of 24 he was gloomy and seclusive, not as much as he currently is, but still enough to the point where people looked at him with a sort of loathing.

The underground man shares his insecurity with us again by talking about how he has always had a feeling of discontent with himself and he, self admittedly, wrongfully assumes this characteristic to others as well. He also talks about how he dislikes his own face, how above all he worries about looking abject and strives for having the face of an intelligent, confident man, whatever that means.

As I continue to read, I continue to resonate with the underground man. His explanations of insecurity and attribution of such to others continues to strike a similar nerve with me. Although it is easy to say that it is not right to assume others to think like this. One cannot help but assume others tend to think like themselves because that is all they know.

The underground man continues by talking about how he dislikes the romanticism of Germany and France, it is too idealistic. Instead, he prefers Russian romanticism where although they are focused on what is good and beautiful in life, they are more realistic.

It seems as if Dostoevsky is, in some sense, writing the underground man to represent Russia. He hates the romanticism that leads him to want to befriend his coworkers, yet he adores Russian romanticism. He becomes friends with his coworkers (lets in western thought), only to cast them aside (become at odds with how much influence western philosophy is affecting him).

One day, depressed and alone, the underground man is walking home when he sees someone being thrown out of a window. Being starved for attention, he decides that he would fancy being thrown out of a window too. Anything is better than being alone. When inside a soldier moves him to the side without any trouble. This sparks a furious desire for revenge.

The desire grew for years, I repeat… years. He stalked him, learned where he lived, how he lived, and with whom he may have lived. His stalking grew to obsession as he saw how the soldier would only move out of the way for higher ranks and no one else. The underground man despised him but was also extremely envious of him at the same time. He soon came up with a master plan.

His plan? To walk directly into the soldier, not get out of his way, and push back hard. He set out by acquiring expensive clothes to look the soldiers equal. The main component, replacing his racoon collar with a German beaver one (more on why I think this is important to come). He eventually gets the courage to do it, but the officer hardly notices.

This whole story, in my opinion, continues to fall in line with Dostoevsky’s commentary on western philosophy coming to Russia. The underground man has too lofty of ideals for Russia and he despises those that can adapt so well to the area as the tough, yet bending, soldier. The collar is important because the underground man talks about how the German beaver collar doesn’t hold up well in the Russian climate, yet it is beautiful while it does. This is again commentary on how western philosophy looks nice and how it may appear beautiful. But it does not translate well to the lifestyle in Russia. Even after he carried it out, the officer hardly noticed his “diabolical” plan that took years for him to think about and act out.

Chapter 2:

After his heroic display of strength and independence in chapter 1. He comes down with what he describes to be a sickness. Now the actual nature of this sickness is debatable and is to be left to the reader to interpret the meaning of. My assumption, the sickness of being underground, being alone, not allowing oneself to truly be vulnerable.

He tells us that he combats this state of degradation by thinking himself to be a hero at times. For a normal man would not accept this. However, a hero knows himself to be greater than a normal man, and thus can defile himself at times without shame.

He describes his dreams, they are western European in origin. Dreams that he describes fondly, yet he cannot bring into the real world.

The underground man’s dreams and his inability to bring them into the real world and act on the feelings that they give him is again Dostoevsky commentating on how western thought does not belong in Russia.

The chapter ends with the underground man talking about how he gets sudden desires to share his genius only to become socially anxious in such situations. In typical fashion, he tells us that all is well and continues to boast about himself.

I am now at odds. At this point it is known that Dostoevsky is using the underground man as a vessel for western European thought as a way to show that it does not belong in Russia. However, what is necessary for displaying the underground man in such a light? Why does it matter that he is depressed and socially anxious.

One answer could be because Dostoevsky really wanted to get across how contemptuous a man can become when one culture tries to adopt another so suddenly. Perhaps these character traits are only put on display for the reader to realize, through obvious dramatization, that we are also victims to the same thinking patterns.

Chapter 3:

The underground man can’t contain himself anymore and visits one of his old friends from school, even though he hated everyone there. On the way there however, he concedes to the reader that even though he despises his old schoolmates, he seeks their respect at the same time.

He describes his old classmate with a sort of stark hatred and resentment. Jealousy because this classmate, Zverkov, was handsome and rich, hatred because he seemed to be aware of this and would “swagger” around the others who could do nothing but bow at his feet. “..simply because he had been favoured by the gifts of nature.”

The three men are planning a party for the friend, none of them acknowledge the underground man, and they make it quite clear that he is not a part of their plans. The underground man however, rudely, interjects himself to be included. After obvious vexation from the others, they concede, and he is allowed to come.

Afterwards, the underground man terribly regrets his actions. He confides in the reader that his reasoning for doing this, which we are aware of at this point, is that he has a desire to put himself in uncomfortable situations. Why? Because that is when he feels truly alive.

The rest of the chapter is the underground man giving us some back story on his time in school, why the other student’s mocked him, how he hates them, how he secretly just wants all of them to like and respect him, the usual stuff.
